## STARTER QUERIES — Run These First

### Query 1: Initial Crowd Response
```
How will the crowd of approximately 22,000 people at L.T. Smith Stadium react during
the first 10 minutes after a TORNADO WARNING is issued via WKU Alert text message
and announced over the stadium PA system? Focus on: how many shelter in place inside
the stadium vs. attempt to exit the building vs. freeze and wait for peer cues.
```

### Query 2: Compliance Rates
```
What percentage of the crowd at a WKU home football game follows official EOP
shelter-in-place instructions when a tornado warning is issued? What percentage
attempts to self-evacuate to parking lots or personal vehicles? What percentage
freezes, shelters in incorrect locations, or waits to see what others do first?
Break down by group: WKU students, visiting fans, families with children, and
tailgaters who were outside the stadium when the warning was issued.
```

### Query 3: Tailgater Vulnerability
```
Simulate the behavior of approximately 3,000–5,000 people currently in outdoor
tailgate areas on the WKU South Lawn and parking lots when a tornado warning is
issued. The nearest shelter (stadium interior) is 200–800 feet away depending on
location. How many make it to shelter within 10 minutes? What obstacles slow
compliance? Where are the most dangerous bottlenecks?
```

## INTERMEDIATE QUERIES — Communication Variables

### Query 4: Alert Delay Impact
```
If campus police make PA announcements inside the stadium immediately, but the
WKU Alert text message is delayed by 8 minutes due to system load, how does that
gap affect crowd movement and compliance for the roughly 5,000 people who are
OUTSIDE the stadium (tailgating, in parking lots, or walking between buildings)?
Compare the outcome to a scenario where WKU Alert fires simultaneously with
the PA announcement.
```

### Query 5: Follow-Up Message Effect
```
How does crowd compliance and panic level change if WKU Alert sends a second,
more specific follow-up message 5 minutes after the initial tornado warning alert?
The follow-up message reads: "TORNADO WARNING — Seek shelter IMMEDIATELY inside
L.T. Smith Stadium lower level, DSU basement, or nearest concrete structure.
Do NOT go to your vehicle. Tornado confirmed 8 miles SW, tracking NE toward campus."
```

### Query 6: Wireless Emergency Alert vs. WKU Alert
```
A federal Wireless Emergency Alert (WEA) fires on every cell phone in Warren County
simultaneously with the tornado warning. How does receiving the WEA on their personal
phone change behavior for visiting fans who are NOT registered for WKU Alert and have
no prior familiarity with campus emergency protocols?
```

## ADVANCED QUERIES — Crowd Dynamics & Misinformation

### Query 7: Misinformation Spread
```
Predict the most likely false rumors or misinformation to emerge in the first 20
minutes of a tornado warning at a full-capacity WKU football game. How does each
rumor spread (person-to-person vs. social media vs. overheard conversation)?
Which false belief is most likely to cause dangerous crowd behavior — such as
people moving from shelter back into the open — and how should WKU's
communication plan counter it?
```

### Query 8: Social Media vs. Official Channels
```
Simulate how information about the tornado warning spreads via student social media
(Twitter/X, Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat) versus official WKU channels (WKU Alert,
stadium PA, campus police) during the first 30 minutes. Which channel reaches more
people faster? At what point does social media information diverge from official
guidance, and what effect does that divergence have on crowd compliance?
```

### Query 9: Most Vulnerable Group
```
Which population group at a WKU game-day tornado event is statistically least likely
to respond correctly to a shelter-in-place order, and why? Consider: freshmen
students (first semester), commuter students, visiting fans from out of state,
elderly attendees, people with disabilities, and people under the influence of
alcohol. For the highest-risk group, what one change to the EOP communication
plan would most improve their response rate?
```

## SCENARIO VARIANT QUERIES — Test Specific Conditions

### Query 10: Warning During Pregame Tailgate
```
The tornado warning is issued 90 minutes before kickoff, when most attendees are
in outdoor tailgate areas and the stadium gates have just opened. How does this
timing change crowd behavior compared to a mid-game warning? Are more or fewer
people in dangerous outdoor positions? How does the festive pregame atmosphere
affect willingness to take the warning seriously?
```

### Query 11: Warning in Final Minutes of Game
```
The tornado warning is issued with 4 minutes left in a close game (WKU trailing
by 3 points). How does the emotional investment in the game outcome compete with
survival instinct and compliance with shelter-in-place instructions? What
percentage of fans refuse to leave their seats immediately?
```

### Query 12: Bottleneck & Crowd Crush Risk
```
If 22,000 people in L.T. Smith Stadium are all directed to shelter in the stadium's
lower concourse level simultaneously, identify the 3 most likely bottleneck points
that could cause dangerous crowd crush conditions. What is the estimated time to
clear each bottleneck under normal vs. panicked crowd movement? What EOP
modifications could reduce crush risk?
```

Based on the simulated crowd behavior during the WKU game-day tornado scenario,
rate the effectiveness of the current EOP communication strategy on a scale of
1–10. What are the top 3 weaknesses in the current protocol? Provide specific,

the next home game season.

game-day scenario — one optimized for speed/brevity, one optimized for clarity

how the crowd's compliance rate and emotional response would differ from the
current standard alert message.
